Automotive Engineering

Drive Axle Load Analysis
Dana Inc. × Shanghai Jiao Tong University January 2025 – April 2025

This capstone project focused on structural durability evaluation and load path optimization of automotive and off highway drive axle systems under realistic operating conditions. The primary objective was to improve durability and NVH assessment fidelity by integrating multi body dynamics simulations with finite element based structural analysis.

Detailed axle geometry was developed in SolidWorks based on sponsor discussions and publicly available design references due to confidentiality limited hollow models provided by Dana Incorporated. Structural stress and strain analysis was performed to identify critical load bearing regions under combined torque bending and operational loads representative of backhoe loader duty cycles.

Multi body dynamics simulations were conducted using MSC ADAMS to generate time varying drivetrain load profiles. These simulations were developed collaboratively with Shanghai Jiao Tong University and later extended independently to ensure full understanding of the motion model and data exchange between ADAMS and SolidWorks based workflows.

Dynamic load histories generated from ADAMS were correlated with structural results to establish a load to life durability workflow. Design level geometry refinements reduced peak stress concentrations by approximately six percent while preserving packaging and functional constraints improving overall durability confidence.

Key technical outcomes
  • Established an integrated CAD MBD FEA durability analysis workflow
  • Evaluated stress strain and load bearing capacity of Dana Spicer and Kramer axles
  • Reduced peak stress concentrations by six percent through geometry refinement
  • Improved correlation between dynamic duty cycles and fatigue life
  • Methodology transferable to ICE and EV drivetrain applications

Piezoelectric Energy Harvesting

Energy Efficient Mining Truck Concept
Penn State University | Industry Sponsored by Wabtec Corporation August 2023 – December 2023

First year engineering design project at Penn State University sponsored by Wabtec Corporation, a US based construction and mining equipment manufacturer. The project addressed fuel inefficiency in large mining dump trucks caused by long haul distances and high fossil fuel consumption during site access.

Evaluated alternative energy concepts including solar and full electrification and identified limitations due to external energy dependence. Proposed a piezoelectric energy harvesting approach leveraging the high ground pressure and mass of mining trucks to generate electrical energy through mechanical deformation.

Developed a concept involving piezoelectric plates embedded along haul paths and within tire structures to enable continuous energy generation during vehicle operation. The system was designed to supplement onboard battery storage and regenerative braking to reduce overall fuel dependency while improving operational efficiency.

Modeled tire deformation behavior in SolidWorks and analyzed strain to electrical output relationships using MATLAB to estimate power generation potential. Results indicated a partially self sustaining system capable of significantly reducing fuel consumption under realistic operating conditions.

Key technical outcomes
  • Developed a piezoelectric based energy harvesting concept for heavy duty vehicles
  • Integrated mechanical loading energy generation and storage concepts
  • Applied CAD modeling and MATLAB based energy estimation
  • Awarded Best Concept Idea at the end of semester project showcase

Aerodynamics

Research on Aerodynamics and Wake Management in Formula 1
January 2023 – August 2023

Independent research project driven by interest in Formula 1 regulations and vehicle safety, focused on understanding wake turbulence generated by leading cars and its impact on following vehicle stability, cornering performance, and overtaking capability.

Studied regulation driven aerodynamic constraints and analyzed laminar and turbulent flow behavior around open wheel race cars. Identified that high energy turbulent wakes generated by the leading car significantly degrade downstream flow quality, reducing downforce and inducing instability for the trailing car at high speeds.

Developed simplified Formula 1 geometry using CATIA and SolidWorks and performed CFD simulations in ANSYS to analyze pressure distributions flow separation regions and wake structures. Iteratively refined front wing floor and rear wing geometries to preserve underbody downforce while reducing wake turbulence intensity downstream.

Rear aerodynamic elements were redesigned to manage vortex shedding and promote cleaner wake structures, resulting in an estimated twenty to thirty percent reduction in wake turbulence intensity while maintaining comparable downforce levels. Structural checks were performed to ensure load bearing capability of aerodynamic components under realistic operating loads.

The research was conducted in collaboration with a PhD candidate and culminated in a co authored technical paper submitted for peer review to a US based scientific journal, Advances in Aerodynamics. The project spanned eight months.

Key technical outcomes
  • Analyzed wake turbulence effects on vehicle stability and overtaking performance
  • Applied CFD to evaluate pressure gradients separation behavior and wake structures
  • Achieved significant wake turbulence reduction while maintaining downforce
  • Integrated aerodynamic design with basic structural validation
  • Gained hands on experience with ANSYS CFD, CATIA, and SolidWorks
